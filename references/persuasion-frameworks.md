# Persuasion Frameworks for Content Creation

These are not academic summaries. Each framework is broken down into how to actually use it when writing hooks, scripts, and content. If you can't apply it in under 60 seconds of video, it's not useful here.

---

## Hormozi's Value Equation

**Dream Outcome x Perceived Likelihood / Time Delay x Effort & Sacrifice**

The goal: make the top of the fraction massive and the bottom tiny. Every piece of content should move at least one of these four levers.

### How to Apply in Hooks

Hooks that work almost always hit one of these:
- **Increase Dream Outcome:** "How I went from $3k/month to $42k/month" — the gap IS the hook
- **Increase Perceived Likelihood:** "I've done this with 147 clients" — proof makes it feel achievable
- **Reduce Time Delay:** "In the next 90 seconds, I'll show you..." — speed is magnetic
- **Reduce Effort/Sacrifice:** "Without spending a dollar on ads" — removing the hard part

Stack two or more for a stronger hook: "How I added $12k/month in 30 days without hiring anyone" hits dream outcome, time delay, AND effort.

### How to Apply in Scripts

- **Proof points increase perceived likelihood.** Don't just claim results — show screenshots, name specific numbers, reference named clients (with permission). "This works" is weak. "This generated $847 in the first 48 hours for a skincare brand" is strong.
- **"Only takes X minutes" reduces time delay.** Break processes into absurdly small first steps. "You can set this up during your lunch break" beats "implement this system."
- **Remove sacrifice by reframing.** Instead of "you need to post every day," try "you need 15 minutes and a phone." Same action, different perceived cost.

### 3 Script Examples

**Fitness niche:**
"Most people think getting lean takes months of meal prep and 6am gym sessions. I dropped 14 pounds in 8 weeks eating at restaurants 4 nights a week. Here's the only rule I followed..."
(Dream outcome high, effort crushed, time delay short, likelihood boosted by specificity)

**B2B niche:**
"My client was spending $4,000/month on lead gen and getting 3 calls a week. We changed one thing on his landing page — took 20 minutes — and he got 11 calls the next week without increasing ad spend."
(Dream outcome = more calls. Effort = one change, 20 minutes. Likelihood = specific client story.)

**Creator niche:**
"I gained 40,000 followers in 90 days. I don't have a ring light. I don't script my videos. I don't even edit them. Here's what I do instead..."
(Dream outcome = followers. Every "I don't" reduces perceived sacrifice.)

## Cialdini's 6 Principles of Influence

### 1. Reciprocity
Give value first, and people feel compelled to give back (follow, subscribe, buy).

**In content:** Teach something genuinely useful before asking for anything. The best creators give away their real playbook, not watered-down versions.

**Example hook:** "I'm going to give you the exact cold email template that booked me 23 meetings last month. Screenshot this."

**Don't do this:** Gating basic information behind "DM me for the answer." People feel manipulated, not grateful.

### 2. Commitment & Consistency
Once someone takes a small action, they're more likely to take a bigger one.

**In content:** Get micro-commitments. "If you've ever felt this way, keep watching." Now they've mentally committed. By the end, "subscribe" feels like a natural next step.

**Example script line:** "If you're a freelancer charging under $5k/month, this is specifically for you. Here's what to change first..."

**Don't do this:** "Like this video RIGHT NOW before I continue." Forced commitment feels hostile, not persuasive.

### 3. Social Proof
People follow what others are doing, especially in uncertainty.

**In content:** Reference numbers, community, and shared experience. "Over 2,000 founders have used this framework" is more persuasive than "this framework works."

**Example hook:** "Every successful creator I know does this one thing that beginners skip."

**Don't do this:** Fake comment screenshots, purchased followers, or "everyone is talking about this" when they aren't. Audiences smell it instantly.

### 4. Authority
People trust credible experts and follow their recommendations.

**In content:** Drop credentials naturally, not as a flex. Weave them into the story. "When I was editing for Netflix, I noticed something..." establishes authority without chest-pounding.

**Example script line:** "After working with 50+ founders on their content, I keep seeing the same three mistakes."

**Don't do this:** Lead with a credential dump. "As a Harvard-educated, TEDx-speaking, 7-figure entrepreneur..." — authority is earned through demonstrated knowledge, not listed.

### 5. Liking
People are persuaded by those they like, relate to, and find similar to themselves.

**In content:** Share failures, behind-the-scenes reality, and opinions. Vulnerability and honesty build liking faster than polish. "I lost $20,000 on this launch" is more likable than "my launches always succeed."

**Example hook:** "I'm going to tell you about the worst business decision I made this year. It cost me a client and $8,000."

**Don't do this:** Manufacture relatability. Posting from a Lamborghini while saying "I'm just like you" destroys trust.

### 6. Scarcity
Limited availability increases perceived value.

**In content:** Use real scarcity — genuine deadlines, actual capacity limits, truly limited offers. "I only take 4 clients at a time because I do the work myself" is real. "Only 3 spots left!!!" every week is not.

**Example script line:** "I'm sharing this now because the platform hasn't caught on to this yet. Give it 6 months and everyone will be doing it."

**Don't do this:** Fake countdown timers, "closing soon" on evergreen offers, or artificial urgency. This is the fastest way to permanently lose credibility.

## StoryBrand (Donald Miller)

**Character** has a **Problem**, meets a **Guide** who gives a **Plan**, calls to **Action**, helps avoid **Failure**, achieves **Success**.

### The Key Insight

The viewer is the hero. You are the guide. This is the single most common mistake in content — creators position themselves as the main character. The viewer doesn't care about your journey unless it teaches them about theirs.

### How Every Piece of Content Uses This Arc

Even a 30-second TikTok follows this unconsciously:
- **Character:** "If you're a freelancer struggling to land clients..." (that's THEM)
- **Problem:** "...you're probably making this one mistake on your proposals..."
- **Guide:** "...I've sent over 300 proposals and here's what I found..." (credibility = guide status)
- **Plan:** "...stop leading with your portfolio. Lead with their problem..."
- **Action:** "...try this on your next 5 proposals..."
- **Failure avoided:** "...instead of getting ghosted..."
- **Success:** "...you'll actually get responses."

### 60-Second Script Template (StoryBrand)

> You know that feeling when [PROBLEM the viewer faces]? You've probably tried [COMMON FAILED SOLUTION] and it didn't work. That's because [ROOT CAUSE — reframe the problem].
>
> I [CREDIBILITY — brief proof you're a valid guide]. And I found that [SIMPLE PLAN — the shift or framework].
>
> Here's what to do: [CLEAR ACTION — specific, not vague].
>
> If you don't [CONSEQUENCE — what failure looks like], but if you do [SUCCESS — paint the outcome].
>
> [CTA — one clear next step].

## PAS (Problem-Agitate-Solve)

The oldest copywriting framework still works because it mirrors how humans make decisions: recognize a problem, feel the urgency, grab the solution.

### Compressed Into a 30-Second Hook

**Problem** (5 seconds): State the pain clearly.
**Agitate** (10 seconds): Twist the knife — show why it's worse than they think.
**Solve** (15 seconds): Present the fix.

**Example (coaching niche):**
"You're posting content every day and getting zero leads. (Problem) The worst part? You're actually training the algorithm to ignore you — every low-engagement post tells the platform your content isn't worth showing. (Agitate) But there's a format shift that fixes this in about a week. (Solve — opens loop)"

### Expanded Into a Full Short-Form Script

**Problem** (first 5 seconds): Hook with the pain point — specific, visceral, recognizable.
**Agitate** (next 15-20 seconds): Layer on consequences. What happens if they don't fix this? What are they missing? Make the status quo feel unacceptable.
**Solve** (remaining time): Deliver the insight, framework, or action step. Be specific. End with a clear takeaway.

### 2 Examples

**E-commerce niche:**
"Your product pages are getting traffic but nobody's buying. (P) Here's what's actually happening — visitors are landing, scrolling for about 4 seconds, and bouncing because your first image looks like every other product on Amazon. You're paying for clicks that never convert. (A) Swap your main product photo for a lifestyle image showing the product in use. One brand I worked with saw a 34% increase in add-to-carts from this single change. (S)"

**SaaS niche:**
"Your free trial conversion rate is probably under 5%. (P) That means for every 100 people who try your product, 95 leave and never come back. You're spending money to acquire users who ghost you. And most of them churned because they never hit their 'aha moment' — they signed up, got confused by your dashboard, and closed the tab. (A) Add a single onboarding checklist with 3 steps that gets them to the core value in under 2 minutes. Notion did this and doubled their activation rate. (S)"

## The Gap (Andrew Davis)

People don't watch content for answers. They watch because they have an unanswered question creating tension in their mind. The gap between "what I know" and "what I need to know" is what keeps them watching.

### How to Use Curiosity Stacking

Don't just open one question — stack multiple. Each new question adds a reason to keep watching.

**Example opening:**
"Last month I fired my highest-paying client. They were paying me $8,000 a month. I'll tell you why in a second, but first — the thing that made me do it was something they said on a Zoom call that most people would've ignored."

Three gaps opened:
1. Why did he fire them?
2. What did the client say?
3. Why would most people ignore it?

The viewer now needs all three answered. They'll watch through mid-roll ads to get them.

### How to Open and Close Loops

**Opening a loop:** Introduce a question, promise, or incomplete story. "There's a third mistake that's worse than both of these, and I'll get to it after this next point."

**Closing a loop:** Deliver the payoff. If you open it, you must close it — unresolved loops frustrate viewers and kill trust for future content.

**Staggering loops:** Open Loop A, open Loop B, close Loop A, open Loop C, close Loop B, close Loop C. This keeps tension constant because there's always at least one unresolved question.

### Why Unanswered Questions Keep People Watching

The brain treats an open question like an incomplete task — it creates low-level cognitive tension that demands resolution (this is the Zeigarnik effect in practice). Use this intentionally:

- **In hooks:** "There's one thing every successful founder does that you've never heard anyone talk about."
- **At transitions:** "But that's not even the most important part."
- **Before CTAs:** "If you want the full breakdown of step three, I made a detailed guide — link in the description."
- **In series content:** End each video with an open loop that the next video closes.

The gap is the single most powerful retention tool in content. Master this and every other framework becomes more effective because people actually stay long enough to hear your message.
