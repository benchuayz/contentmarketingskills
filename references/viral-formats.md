# Viral Content Formats & Platform Rules

A practical reference for writing platform-native content. Every platform has different rules, attention spans, and audience expectations. Content that works on TikTok will flop on LinkedIn if you don't adapt.

---

## Part 1: Platform-Specific Rules

### TikTok
- **Video length:** 30-90 seconds is the sweet spot. Under 60 seconds for maximum completion rate. Videos over 3 minutes can work but need strong retention hooks.
- **Tone:** Raw, casual, fast-paced. Over-produced content underperforms. Speak like you're talking to a friend, not presenting to a boardroom.
- **Captions:** Keep on-screen text short (5-8 words per line). Use captions/subtitles always — most people scroll with sound off first.
- **Description:** Under 300 characters works best. Front-load the hook in the first line. Don't waste space on hashtag walls.
- **Hashtags:** 3-5 relevant hashtags max. Mix broad (#business) with niche (#freelancertips). Skip trending hashtags that have nothing to do with your content.
- **First 1-3 seconds:** Pattern interrupt or you're dead. Movement, a bold statement, unexpected visual, or direct eye contact with a provocative claim.

### Instagram Reels
- **Video length:** 15-60 seconds. Instagram rewards Reels that get replayed, so shorter often performs better.
- **Tone:** Slightly more polished than TikTok but still authentic. Instagram audiences expect marginally higher production value.
- **Captions (post text):** Up to 2,200 characters, but front-load value in the first 2 lines (before the "more" cut). Use this space for context the video doesn't cover.
- **Carousel vs Reel:** Carousels for educational/list content (higher saves). Reels for personality-driven, story, or trend content (higher reach). If the content is better as text + images, make it a carousel.
- **Hashtags:** 5-10 relevant ones. Put them at the end of the caption or in a comment.
- **Cover image:** Matters more here than TikTok. Design a thumbnail with 3-5 words of text overlay.

### YouTube Shorts
- **Video length:** Up to 60 seconds. Aim for 30-50 seconds for optimal completion rate.
- **How it differs from TikTok:** YouTube Shorts audiences skew slightly older and more intentional. Value-dense content outperforms pure entertainment. Viewers are more likely to subscribe from a Short than from a TikTok.
- **Vertical format:** 9:16. No horizontal video cropped to vertical — it looks bad and underperforms.
- **Hook:** Same urgency as TikTok but lean harder into "here's something useful" vs. "here's something entertaining."
- **End screen:** No end screen on Shorts. Your CTA needs to be verbal, in the last 5 seconds.
- **Title:** Treat it like a headline. Under 50 characters. The title is visible in search and recommendations.

### LinkedIn
- **Length:** 150-300 words. Under 150 feels thin. Over 400 loses people.
- **Tone:** Founder-to-founder. Peer-level, not guru-level. Direct, results-focused, slightly contrarian.
- **Formatting:** Line breaks after every 1-2 sentences. No walls of text. Start with a punchy first line (it's the hook before "see more").
- **Emojis:** Max 2-3 per post. Zero is fine. Never use emoji bullets for a whole list.
- **What NOT to do:** No generic listicles ("5 tips for..."), no marketing jargon ("leverage," "synergy," "unlock"), no humble-bragging disguised as advice, no "I'm grateful/blessed" posts.
- **What works:** Specific numbers, honest failures, contrarian takes backed by experience, short stories with a clear takeaway.

### Twitter/X
- **Tweets:** Under 280 characters. Punchy. Fragmented. One idea per tweet.
- **Threads:** 3-7 tweets. First tweet is the hook (must stand alone). Last tweet is the CTA. Each tweet should deliver value independently — don't spread one sentence across three tweets.
- **Tone:** Sharp, opinionated, concise. Write like you're texting someone smart. No filler words.
- **What NOT to do:** Don't over-expand an idea that should be one sentence. Don't use threads for content that should be a blog post. Don't use emojis as bullet points.
- **What works:** Bold claims, specific numbers, short stories (2-3 sentences), and "the real reason" framing.

### Newsletter
- **Length:** 300-500 words for quick-hit newsletters. Up to 1,500 for deep-dive formats.
- **Structure:** Personal story or observation (2-3 paragraphs) into a clear insight or lesson into one actionable takeaway into CTA.
- **Tone:** Conversational, like an email from a smart friend. First-person, not editorial.
- **Subject line:** Under 50 characters. Curiosity or specificity. "How I lost $12k last month" beats "Monthly Business Update."
- **CTA:** One CTA at the end. Don't scatter multiple CTAs throughout — it dilutes action.

## Part 2: Content Format Archetypes

### 1. The Contrarian Take
Challenge conventional wisdom with evidence. "Everyone says X. Here's why that's wrong."
**When to use:** You have genuine experience that contradicts popular advice. Not for contrarian-for-the-sake-of-it takes.
**Structure:** Bold claim (hook) > why people believe the opposite > your evidence/experience > the reframe > takeaway.

### 2. The Step-by-Step Breakdown
Walk through an exact process in numbered steps. "Here's exactly how to do [thing], step by step."
**When to use:** You have a repeatable process that gets specific results and can be explained concisely.
**Structure:** End result teaser > prerequisite (if any) > steps 1-3 (or 1-5) with brief explanation each > common mistake to avoid > result.

### 3. The Story with a Lesson
A personal narrative that ends with a transferable insight. "Last year, something happened that changed how I..."
**When to use:** You have a real experience (success or failure) that illustrates a principle better than theory.
**Structure:** Scene-setting (brief) > tension/conflict > turning point > lesson > how the viewer applies it.

### 4. The "Here's What Nobody Tells You"
Reveal hidden information or insider knowledge. Feels exclusive.
**When to use:** You genuinely have insight that isn't commonly shared — industry secrets, behind-the-scenes, or overlooked details.
**Structure:** Setup the common narrative > "but here's what actually happens" > reveal the hidden truth > why it matters > what to do about it.

### 5. The Before/After Reveal
Show transformation with contrast. Visual or narrative.
**When to use:** You have clear, demonstrable results. Works for portfolios, client work, fitness, design, business metrics.
**Structure:** Show/describe the "before" > what was wrong > what changed > show/describe the "after" > the specific action that caused the shift.

### 6. The Expert Reaction
React to content, trends, news, or someone else's take with expert commentary.
**When to use:** Something in your industry is trending and you have a qualified, non-obvious opinion.
**Structure:** Show the thing > initial reaction > deeper analysis > what most people miss > your actual take.

### 7. The "I Tested This So You Don't Have To"
Document an experiment with real results. Data-driven.
**When to use:** You actually ran the experiment. Don't fabricate this.
**Structure:** What you tested > why > the method (brief) > results with numbers > surprising findings > verdict and recommendation.

### 8. The Framework Drop
Introduce a mental model or system that simplifies a complex topic.
**When to use:** You've developed (or adapted) a framework that genuinely helps people think about a problem differently.
**Structure:** The problem this solves > the framework name/concept > each component explained briefly > example of it in action > how to start using it.

### 9. The Industry Callout
Call out a broken practice, bad advice, or industry standard that harms people.
**When to use:** You have standing to criticize (experience in the space) and a better alternative to offer.
**Structure:** The problematic practice > why it exists > who it hurts > what to do instead > your proof it works better.

### 10. The Real Numbers Reveal
Share actual financial, performance, or growth data with transparency.
**When to use:** You have real numbers to share and they tell an interesting or useful story. Not for vanity metrics.
**Structure:** The number(s) up front > context (what this means, what it took) > breakdown of what drove it > what you'd do differently > what the viewer can learn.

### 11. The Prediction/Trend Analysis
Forecast where things are heading based on patterns you're seeing.
**When to use:** You have enough experience to spot patterns and your audience cares about staying ahead.
**Structure:** The trend > evidence you're seeing > why most people haven't noticed > what it means for the viewer > how to position for it.

### 12. The "One Thing" Simplification
Distill a complex topic into one actionable takeaway. "If you only do one thing..."
**When to use:** When a topic is overcomplicated and people need permission to focus on what matters most.
**Structure:** The overwhelming topic > why most advice makes it worse > the one thing that moves the needle > why it works > exactly how to do it.

## Part 3: Universal Content Principles

**Value density over length.** A 30-second video with one clear insight beats a 3-minute video with filler. Every sentence should earn its place. If removing a line doesn't weaken the piece, remove it.

**Specificity over generality.** "I made $4,200 from one email" beats "email marketing works." Numbers, names, timeframes, and details make content believable and memorable. Vague advice is forgettable advice.

**Pattern interrupt in the first 3 seconds.** You're competing with the next swipe. The opening must stop the scroll — a bold claim, an unexpected visual, a direct question, movement, or tension. "Hey guys, welcome back" is a death sentence.

**One core idea per piece.** Don't try to cover everything. A video about "3 ways to grow on LinkedIn" is weaker than "the one LinkedIn hack that got me 12 inbound leads." Depth beats breadth in short-form. Save the other ideas for more content.

**End with a clear takeaway or question.** The viewer should walk away knowing exactly what to do next, or thinking about a question that keeps your content in their mind. "So next time you write a proposal, lead with their problem, not your portfolio" is clear. "Anyway, that's what I think" is wasted real estate.
