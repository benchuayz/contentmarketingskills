## Script 1 — The Efficiency Argument

I work out 3 hours a week. Total. And I'm in better shape than I was training 6 days a week for 90 minutes.

That sounds like a lie. But I tracked everything for 18 months across 340 clients, and the data is clear. For people with demanding desk jobs, 3 focused sessions per week beats 6 unfocused ones every single time.

Here's why. When you train 6 days a week, you're always slightly tired. Your form breaks down. Your intensity drops. You spend half your workout recovering from yesterday's workout. And mentally, it becomes this thing you dread because it never stops.

But 3 sessions? You show up rested. You push hard. You recover fully between sessions. And you actually have a life outside the gym.

I was the guy doing 5am workouts, 6-day splits, protein shakes at my standing desk. My body barely changed. The moment I cut my training in half and focused on progressive overload in just 5 compound movements, everything shifted.

More isn't better. Better is better. And for anyone working 50-plus hours a week, that's not just advice — it's math.

## Script 2 — The Environment Design

Your fitness problem isn't discipline. It's architecture.

Every tech company in the world knows that if you want people to use a feature, you make it easy to find. You don't hide the sign-up button and hope users are motivated enough to search for it.

Your fitness is the same thing.

When I was an engineer at Stripe, I tried every motivation hack. Vision boards. Accountability partners. Alarm clocks across the room. None of it lasted more than a month.

Then I did something different. I stopped trying to be more disciplined and started designing my environment. Gym bag in my car, not my closet. Pre-made protein shakes in the fridge, not ingredients I'd have to prepare. A pull-up bar in my doorframe that I use every time I walk past.

I removed friction from the healthy choices and added friction to the unhealthy ones. No snacks in the house. Phone charges in the other room at 10pm.

Within 3 months, I was in the best shape of my life. Not because I was more motivated. Because I made the default choice the healthy choice.

Stop trying to hack your willpower. Start engineering your environment.

## Script 3 — The Technical Debt Analogy

Your body is running on technical debt.

If you're an engineer, you know exactly what I mean. You ship fast, cut corners, skip the refactoring. It works for a while. Then one day the whole system starts breaking and you can't figure out why.

That's what happens to your body after years at a desk. You skip sleep to ship a feature. You eat garbage during a product launch. You sit 12 hours straight because you're in flow state. Each one is a small shortcut. But they compound.

By 28, I was pre-diabetic. Not because I was eating fast food every day. Because I'd spent 4 years making small compromises that stacked up. Technical debt — but for my body.

The fix is the same too. You don't rewrite the whole system overnight. You refactor gradually. Small, consistent improvements. 15 minutes of walking after lunch. One more serving of protein per day. 30 minutes less screen time before bed.

340 clients later, I can tell you — the ones who try to do a complete rewrite fail. The ones who refactor win. Every time.

Your body isn't broken. It just needs a refactor. Start small. Ship it. Iterate.
